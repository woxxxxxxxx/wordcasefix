Color Theory Basics: HEX, RGB, HSL Explained for Designers

By WordCaseFix  ·  9 min read  ·  Design

Every digital designer eventually needs to fluently move between the three dominant color notations on the web: HEX, RGB, and HSL. They all describe the same colors, but they serve very different purposes. HEX is compact and copy-pasteable, RGB maps directly to how screens emit light, and HSL is the only model that lets you reason about color the way humans actually perceive it. Choosing the right model for the task in front of you is the difference between fighting a color system and shipping one.

This guide walks through the math behind each notation, when to prefer one over the others, and the accessibility implications. You will also see how to convert between them, both manually and with conversion tools.

The RGB Model: How Screens Actually Work

RGB stands for red, green, blue — the three primary colors of additive light. Every pixel on your monitor is composed of three sub-pixels, one for each channel, and each sub-pixel can emit a brightness from 0 (off) to 255 (maximum). With 256 possible values per channel and three channels, the total color space is 256 × 256 × 256 = 16,777,216 distinct colors.

RGB is the "native" model in the sense that it corresponds to physical hardware. When you write rgb(255, 0, 0) in CSS, you are telling the screen to fire the red sub-pixel at full intensity and leave the others off. RGB is great for programmatic color work — animation, image processing, generative art — because the three numbers map cleanly to channel arithmetic.

What RGB is bad at is human reasoning. If I show you rgb(120, 80, 200) and ask "what does that look like?", you probably can't picture it. RGB obscures hue, saturation, and lightness behind three raw channel values.

HEX: Compact and Copy-Paste-Friendly

HEX is just RGB written in base 16. Each pair of hex digits represents one of the three channels, in the order red, green, blue. So #FF0000 is rgb(255, 0, 0), because FF in hex equals 255 in decimal. The popular three-digit shorthand like #F00 expands to #FF0000 by duplicating each digit.

HEX to RGB worked example:
Color: #3A7BD5
Red: 3A = 3×16 + 10 = 58
Green: 7B = 7×16 + 11 = 123
Blue: D5 = 13×16 + 5 = 213
Result: rgb(58, 123, 213)

The advantage of HEX is that it is short, unambiguous, and easy to share. Designers paste #5B5BD6 in Slack rather than rgb(91, 91, 214). The disadvantage is that it does not natively support alpha (transparency) until you use the 8-digit form #5B5BD6FF, which most tools support but many people don't know about. You can quickly switch direction with a dedicated HEX to RGB converter or the reverse RGB to HEX converter.

HSL: The Designer-Friendly Model

HSL stands for hue, saturation, and lightness. It is a cylindrical reparametrization of the RGB cube designed to match how humans talk about color. The hue is an angle from 0 to 360 around the color wheel — red sits at 0, green at 120, blue at 240. Saturation is a percentage from 0 (gray) to 100 (vivid). Lightness is a percentage from 0 (black) to 100 (white), with 50 being the "pure" version of the hue.

HSL becomes powerful the moment you want to build a theme. Want a darker version of your brand color? Lower the L. Want a muted version? Lower the S. Want the complementary color? Add 180 to the H. These operations are nearly impossible to perform mentally in RGB.

GoalHSL operationExample
Darker shadeReduce lightnesshsl(240, 60%, 50%) → hsl(240, 60%, 30%)
Lighter tintIncrease lightnesshsl(240, 60%, 50%) → hsl(240, 60%, 80%)

ComplementaryHue + 180hsl(240, 60%, 50%) → hsl(60, 60%, 50%)
Analogous paletteHue ± 30hsl(240) + hsl(210) + hsl(270)

When constructing palettes, generate the base hue in HSL and convert to HEX at the end for storage. A palette generator can automate this entire workflow.

Accessibility and Contrast

None of these color models, by themselves, guarantee accessibility. WCAG 2.1 requires a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for normal body text and 3:1 for large text. The contrast ratio is calculated from the relative luminance of foreground and background — which is an RGB-based formula, not HSL. A color with low lightness in HSL is not automatically high-contrast against white.

For example, pure yellow hsl(60, 100%, 50%) looks visually bright but has a contrast ratio against white of only about 1.07:1 — completely unreadable. Meanwhile, dark blue hsl(240, 100%, 30%) hits roughly 13:1 against white. Always check the actual ratio with a contrast checker before approving body text colors.

Rule of thumb: design your theme in HSL, store the final colors as HEX, and verify accessibility against the RGB-based contrast formula. All three models earn their place.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I use HEX or HSL in my CSS?

Use HSL for design tokens you intend to manipulate programmatically (themes, dark mode variants, hover states) and HEX or RGB for static brand colors. Modern CSS supports HSL natively in custom properties, so there is no performance reason to avoid it.

What about HSV and other models like LCH?

HSV (sometimes called HSB) is similar to HSL but defines lightness differently — in HSV, 100% V is the pure hue, not white. LCH and OKLCH are perceptually uniform models that produce more visually consistent palettes than HSL, and they are gradually gaining browser support. For most production work, HSL is still the practical default.

Why do my colors look different in print?

Print uses CMYK, a subtractive model that mixes pigments rather than light. Many vivid screen colors — especially saturated blues and greens — cannot be reproduced in CMYK. If your work will be printed, convert to CMYK in your design tool and review proofs before approving.
