Warranty vs Insurance: Key Differences

Homeowners insurance covers sudden and accidental damage from covered perils — fire, storms, theft, and liability. A home warranty covers normal wear and tear on systems and appliances — HVAC, plumbing, electrical, refrigerator, dishwasher, etc. These products are complementary, not interchangeable. Insurance is typically required by lenders; warranties are optional. Warranties are most valuable for older homes where major system or appliance failures are more likely. Always read the fine print — warranty companies frequently deny claims citing pre-existing conditions or improper maintenance.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does a home warranty cover that insurance doesn't?

Home warranties cover mechanical breakdown of appliances and systems due to age and normal use — things homeowners insurance explicitly excludes. This includes HVAC failures, plumbing breaks from wear, and appliance breakdowns.

Can I have both homeowners insurance and a home warranty?

Yes, and many homeowners do. They serve different purposes and together provide more comprehensive financial protection than either product alone.

Are home warranties worth the money?

It depends on your home's age and appliance condition. For older homes with aging systems, warranties can pay off quickly. For newer homes, the cost may exceed the likely repair bills. Always compare coverage limits and exclusions carefully.

What are common home warranty exclusions?

Most home warranties exclude pre-existing conditions, cosmetic issues, code violations, improper installation, and items not specifically listed in the contract. Read the contract in full before purchasing.

Does a home warranty transfer to new owners?

Most home warranties are transferable, which can be a selling point when listing your home. Confirm transferability and any associated fees with your warranty provider before closing.
