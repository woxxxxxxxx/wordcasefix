Why Paying Annually Saves Money

Most insurance companies charge an installment fee — typically 2–5% — when you choose to pay monthly instead of in full. This fee compensates the insurer for the administrative cost and credit risk of spreading payments across 12 months.

While the monthly option improves cash flow, it costs more in total. If you have the funds available, paying annually is almost always the better financial choice. Some insurers also offer a small pay-in-full discount on top of avoiding the installment surcharge.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do installment fees typically cost?

Installment fees vary by insurer but typically range from 2% to 5% of the annual premium, or sometimes a flat fee of $5–$10 per month. On a $1,600 annual premium at 3%, that's $48 extra per year just for the convenience of monthly payments.

Can I switch from monthly to annual payments mid-policy?

Yes, most insurers allow you to pay the remaining balance in full at any time. Doing so will stop future installment fees. Contact your insurer or log into your account to make a lump-sum payment.

Do all insurers charge installment fees?

Not all. Some insurers offer fee-free monthly payments, especially if you set up automatic bank drafts (EFT). Ask your insurer specifically whether they charge for monthly payments and whether auto-pay reduces or eliminates the fee.

What happens if I miss a monthly payment?

Missing a payment can result in a late fee, policy cancellation, or a lapse in coverage. A lapse can lead to higher rates when you reinstate coverage and may be required to be reported to your lender if you have a financed vehicle.

Is there a pay-in-full discount separate from avoiding installment fees?

Yes, some insurers offer an additional pay-in-full discount of 5–10% for paying annually upfront. This is separate from simply avoiding the installment fee. Ask your insurer whether both apply — the combined savings can be significant.
