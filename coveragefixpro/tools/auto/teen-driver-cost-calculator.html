Why Teen Drivers Cost More to Insure

Teen drivers — especially 16- and 17-year-olds — have the highest crash rates of any age group. Insurers reflect this elevated risk with substantially higher premiums. Male teens tend to pay more than female teens due to statistically higher accident rates among young male drivers.

As teens gain experience and maintain a clean record, their rates typically drop significantly by age 25. In the meantime, strategies like good student discounts, driver's education courses, and choosing a safe, older vehicle can help manage costs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it cheaper to add a teen to my policy or get them their own?

In almost all cases, adding a teen to your existing policy is significantly cheaper than purchasing a separate policy for them. Teens benefit from the lower base rate of your existing policy and any multi-car or loyalty discounts you have.

What is the good student discount?

Most major insurers offer a good student discount (typically 5–15%) for teen drivers who maintain a B average or better in school. You'll need to provide proof such as a report card or transcript each year to keep the discount.

Does a driver's education course reduce teen insurance costs?

Yes. Completing an approved driver's education or defensive driving course can reduce premiums by 5–10% with many insurers. Some states also require it for teens to get their license.

Which car should my teen drive to minimize insurance costs?

Older, lower-value vehicles with good safety ratings tend to cost the least to insure. Avoid sports cars, high-performance vehicles, and luxury cars, which carry much higher premiums for teen drivers.

When will my teen's insurance rates go down?

Rates typically decrease noticeably at ages 18–19 and again at 21, with the biggest drop usually coming at age 25 — provided the teen maintains a clean driving record throughout.
