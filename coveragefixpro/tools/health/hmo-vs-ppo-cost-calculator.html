About the HMO vs PPO Calculator

Choosing between an HMO and PPO is one of the most important health insurance decisions you will make. HMOs (Health Maintenance Organizations) generally offer lower premiums and copays but require you to use a network of doctors and get referrals to see specialists. PPOs (Preferred Provider Organizations) cost more but give you greater flexibility to see any doctor, including specialists, without a referral, and provide some coverage for out-of-network care. This calculator helps you compare the true annual cost of each plan type based on your expected healthcare usage. If you rarely see specialists and don't need out-of-network care, an HMO may save you hundreds or thousands per year. If you have ongoing specialist care or value provider flexibility, a PPO's extra cost may be worthwhile.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do HMOs cover any out-of-network care?

Generally only for emergencies. HMOs typically do not cover non-emergency out-of-network care, which can result in very high costs if you need to see a provider outside the network.

Do I need a referral to see a specialist with a PPO?

No. PPOs allow you to self-refer to specialists without going through a primary care physician first. This is one of the main advantages of PPO plans for people with complex health needs.

Are HMO premiums always lower than PPO?

In most cases yes, HMO premiums are lower because HMOs control costs through network restrictions and care coordination. However, the gap varies by plan and region.

What is an EPO and how does it compare?

An Exclusive Provider Organization (EPO) is a hybrid — it has a network like an HMO but doesn't require referrals like a PPO. It typically doesn't cover out-of-network care except in emergencies.

Which plan type is better for a healthy young person?

An HMO is often the better choice for healthy individuals who rarely need specialist care, as the lower premiums provide savings without significant downsides given minimal healthcare usage.
