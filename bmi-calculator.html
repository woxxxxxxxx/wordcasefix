Common Use Cases

  • Health screening: Primary care nurses compute BMI as an initial obesity screen during routine annual physicals.
  • Insurance underwriting: Life insurance applications use BMI as one factor in risk class assignment alongside blood pressure and cholesterol.
  • Personal fitness tracking: Individuals monitor BMI alongside weight to set ranges, though body fat percentage is more accurate for athletes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the formula for BMI?
BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)^2. For pounds and inches: BMI = (weight_lb / height_in^2) * 703. A 70 kg person 1.75 m tall has BMI 22.9, in the WHO "normal weight" range of 18.5-24.9.
What are the BMI categories?
WHO classifies: under 18.5 underweight, 18.5-24.9 normal, 25-29.9 overweight, 30-34.9 obesity class I, 35-39.9 class II, 40+ class III. These cutoffs are based on Caucasian populations; some Asian guidelines use lower thresholds (overweight starts at 23).
Why is BMI inaccurate for athletes?
BMI doesn't distinguish muscle from fat. A 1.85 m, 100 kg bodybuilder has BMI 29.2 ("overweight") despite low body fat. For muscular populations, body fat percentage via DEXA or skinfold calipers is a better measure than BMI alone.
